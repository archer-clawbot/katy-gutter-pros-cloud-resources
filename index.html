A repeatable method

Start with evidence, not a package

Proposal comparison becomes more useful when each provider receives the same observations and the written scopes describe the same work. The objective is not to design the system from a checklist. It is to expose gaps that require measurement, inspection, or a written clarification.

  1. Document the symptom.Use safe ground-level photos and note where and when overflow, leakage, staining, sagging, or ponding appears. Avoid climbing during wet or windy conditions.
  2. Map what feeds each run.Roof area, pitch, valleys, inside corners, upper-roof discharge, and run length can concentrate flow. Ask the inspecting provider to show the measurements behind a recommendation.
  3. Trace outlets and downspouts.Record count, placement, dimensions, elbows, offsets, transitions, restrictions, and each visible endpoint. A gutter cannot be evaluated separately from the openings that drain it.
  4. Specify the proposed system.Capture profile, nominal size, material, finish, hangers, fasteners, attachment spacing, corners, guards, removal, disposal, cleanup, and responsibility for related repairs.
  5. Compare equivalent scopes.Align inclusions, exclusions, payment terms, change-order procedures, and written warranties before comparing totals. Ask for revisions when a material decision is only verbal.

Reading the results

A complete scope is not the same as a qualified provider

The worksheets are designed to make missing information visible. A proposal with more written detail is easier to compare, but that does not establish legal identity, current qualifications, insurance, workmanship, availability, or suitability for a particular property. Verify those items directly and decide what evidence is appropriate for the work. When underground drainage, grading, roofing, fascia, soffit, electrical equipment, or structural conditions enter the conversation, confirm who is responsible and whether another trade should evaluate that part of the project.

Keep the final written scope with photographs, product documents, change orders, payment records, and warranty documents. If an assumption changes, ask for the effect on materials, labor, drainage, exclusions, and total price in writing. The goal is a traceable decision record rather than an unverified promise.
