Property-input checklist

Prepare for a measured gutter scope in Katy

A useful proposal describes one connected water path: roof catchment, gutter run, outlets, downspouts, and final discharge. This guide helps organize the questions; an inspecting provider must verify the property.

Publisher disclosure: Katy Gutter Pros is an independent gutter-information and contractor-matching publisher, not a gutter contractor. It does not inspect properties, prepare contractor proposals, employ crews, or perform gutter work.

Begin with the visible condition

Write down what can be observed safely from the ground. Useful notes include missing coverage, overflow, leaking corners or end caps, standing water, sagging, separation, staining, repeated debris, damaged elbows, disconnected extensions, or discharge that collects in an unwanted area. Record which elevation is involved and whether the symptom appears during light rain, heavy rain, after debris accumulation, or after another roof-edge change. Photos taken from safe locations can help an inspecting provider understand the question, but a photo is not a substitute for measurements or an inspection.

Avoid turning the observation into a diagnosis. Overflow may involve catchment, concentrated valley flow, slope, debris, outlet capacity, downspout restrictions, or a downstream discharge problem. A leak may be isolated, or it may be one sign of wider material or attachment issues. Ask the provider to state the observed cause, the proposed correction, and what remains uncertain.

Map the roof sections that feed each run

Interior floor area does not establish gutter footage or roof catchment. Ask for measured eave lengths by elevation and for the roof sections that drain to each run. Note pitch, valleys, inside corners, dormers, upper-roof discharge, long runs, and short sections that receive concentrated flow. These inputs help explain why two elevations on the same house may need different outlet locations or other details.

The Katy Gutter Pros installation planning guide provides a canonical overview of the installation-scope questions. For field-formed runs, Katy Gutter Pros explains why “seamless” does not remove end caps, corners, outlets, or every transition from the written specification.

Follow water through outlets and downspouts

Record outlet count, dimensions, and placement. An otherwise adequate run can still be limited by too few practical outlets or by an opening that does not match the proposed downspout path. For each downspout, document dimensions, elbows, offsets, straps, transitions, visible restrictions, extensions, and the endpoint. If a proposal references an underground connection, ask whether its condition was evaluated and whether cleaning, testing, repair, or replacement is included.

Roof-water conveyance and site drainage overlap, but they are not automatically the same scope. Grading, underground piping, pumps, foundation drainage, and municipal requirements may require different expertise. The proposal should state where gutter work ends and who is responsible for any related trade.

Turn product labels into comparable specifications

Record gutter profile, nominal size, material, thickness or gauge when applicable, finish, color, hanger type, fasteners, attachment spacing, corner treatment, and outlet details. If guards are proposed, list the product, attachment method, compatibility, maintenance expectations, and price separately. If existing materials will be removed, note disposal, property protection, cleanup, and whether fascia, soffit, drip-edge, roofing, or painting work is included or excluded.

When size is part of the decision, ask the inspecting provider to connect the recommendation to measured roof catchment, pitch, valleys, run length, outlet placement, downspout capacity, and discharge. If the existing system has leaks, sagging, separation, or repeated overflow, use the Katy Gutter Pros repair-planning framework to separate an isolated defect from a broader system question.

Compare scope before total

Place proposals side by side and identify where they describe different work. One may include more measured footage, additional outlets, larger downspouts, removal, related repairs, guards, extensions, cleanup, or different warranty terms. Ask for written clarification instead of assuming that a higher or lower total represents the same deliverable. Use the hub’s downloadable worksheet or interactive comparator to make omissions visible.

After the physical scope aligns, compare totals, taxes, deposits, payment timing, change-order procedures, product-warranty issuer, workmanship terms, and exclusions. The hub’s interactive comparator and CSV worksheet can expose missing fields without rating providers or prices. Do not rely on a verbal promise for a material term. Keep the accepted proposal, amendments, product documents, photographs, payment records, and completion notes together.

Verify the provider separately

Scope completeness does not establish provider identity, qualifications, insurance, availability, workmanship, or suitability. Verify the legal business name, direct contact information, service area, current qualifications, insurance evidence appropriate to the work, references or other evidence you consider relevant, and contract terms directly. Bring a concise question set

  • Which measurements and roof conditions support the recommendation?
  • What is included, excluded, optional, or dependent on further inspection?
  • How do outlets, downspouts, and discharge match the proposed runs?
  • Who handles roof-edge, fascia, soffit, grading, or underground work if it is found?
  • Which written terms change if hidden conditions alter the scope?
